## 1. Purpose and boundary
Traditional Number 1 descriptions often connect blocked agency, challenged authority, delayed progress and public correction with impatience, pride, anger, control or withdrawal. These are traditional interpretive themes, not scientifically verified effects of a birth date.

This unit converts those themes into a conditional, behaviour-specific and non-diagnostic stress architecture. It must never infer trauma, aggression, burnout, psychiatric illness, nervous-system type or fixed coping style from Moolank.

Psychological and occupational-health research is used only to define stress, appraisal, coping, recovery, cumulative load and safer app practices.

## 2. Core stress model
Stress response is treated as an interaction among:
- demand or event;
- appraisal of threat, loss, challenge or injustice;
- perceived control and coping resources;
- bodily and emotional activation;
- coping behaviour;
- social and structural conditions;
- recovery opportunities;
- cumulative load over time.

The same stressor can produce different responses across people, contexts and time.

## 3. Essential distinctions

### Stressor vs stress response
A stressor is a demand. A stress response is the changing cognitive, emotional, bodily and behavioural reaction to that demand.

### Activation vs dysregulation
Short-term activation can be adaptive. Difficulty returning to a workable state may require support, but cannot be diagnosed from one app interaction.

### Control-seeking vs effective control
Control-seeking reduces uncertainty. Effective control targets variables the person can actually influence.

### Anger vs aggression
Anger is an emotion. Aggression is harmful or intimidating behaviour. One does not prove the other.

### Withdrawal vs recovery pause
A recovery pause is explicit, time-bound and followed by re-engagement. Punitive or indefinite silence is different.

### Persistence vs rigidity
Persistence responds to evidence. Rigidity continues mainly to avoid shame, uncertainty or loss of status.

### Independence vs isolation
Healthy independence can include support. Stress-linked isolation hides burden or treats help as weakness.

### Burnout risk vs diagnosis
Chronic unmanaged workplace stress may create exhaustion, mental distance or reduced efficacy. The app may discuss risk factors, but cannot diagnose burnout.

### Fight/flight/freeze shorthand
These are simplified educational labels, not complete clinical categories. Real responses can combine mobilisation, avoidance, shutdown, appeasement, problem-solving and support seeking.

## 4. Fourteen stress-response dimensions

### 4.1 Threat and challenge appraisal
Balanced:
- separates inconvenience from danger;
- identifies whether autonomy, competence, status, belonging or safety feels threatened;
- updates appraisal with evidence.

Overextended:
- treats delay, disagreement or correction as major threat;
- interprets ambiguity as disrespect;
- escalates before checking facts.

Underexpressed:
- minimises serious strain;
- ignores warning signs;
- normalises overload.

### 4.2 Perceived control calibration
Balanced:
- separates control, influence and acceptance;
- acts on controllable variables;
- tolerates remaining uncertainty.

Overextended:
- micromanages;
- takes responsibility for others' choices;
- expands checking and rules.

Underexpressed:
- assumes nothing can change;
- waits passively despite available options.

### 4.3 Early-signal awareness
Balanced:
- notices tension, urgency, irritability, narrowed attention and fatigue before escalation.

Overextended:
- anxiously monitors every sensation.

Underexpressed:
- notices stress only after conflict, shutdown or exhaustion.

### 4.4 Urgency and action impulse
Balanced:
- acts quickly when delay is costly;
- pauses for irreversible choices;
- communicates a real deadline.

Overextended:
- acts mainly to end discomfort;
- pressures others to match internal urgency.

Underexpressed:
- delays low-risk action while seeking certainty.

### 4.5 Irritability and frustration handling
Balanced:
- names frustration proportionately;
- targets process, not personal worth;
- uses requests and redesign.

Overextended:
- becomes commanding, cutting or contemptuous;
- interprets mistakes as disrespect.

Underexpressed:
- suppresses frustration until outburst;
- agrees outwardly while resisting indirectly.

### 4.6 Control expansion and micromanagement
Balanced:
- adds temporary structure where risk requires it;
- defines outcomes, constraints and checkpoints;
- returns ownership after stability improves.

Overextended:
- repeatedly checks or rewrites work;
- reverses delegation;
- creates approval bottlenecks.

Underexpressed:
- delegates without clarity or support.

### 4.7 Defensive pride and status protection
Balanced:
- separates feedback from humiliation;
- acknowledges mistakes without global self-attack;
- protects dignity without denying facts.

Overextended:
- argues to avoid losing face;
- rejects feedback because of delivery;
- retaliates, overexplains or withdraws.

Underexpressed:
- collapses into shame;
- erases valid contribution;
- over-apologises.

### 4.8 Cognitive narrowing and certainty seeking
Balanced:
- simplifies priorities while preserving complexity;
- checks assumptions;
- seeks dissent.

Overextended:
- sees one solution only;
- treats uncertainty as incompetence;
- uses absolute language.

Underexpressed:
- generates endless options without commitment.

### 4.9 Work intensification and overfunctioning
Balanced:
- uses short, bounded increases in effort;
- protects sleep and recovery;
- checks whether extra effort works.

Overextended:
- works to avoid vulnerable feelings;
- takes over others' responsibilities;
- treats rest as weakness.

Underexpressed:
- disengages before testing workable adjustments.

### 4.10 Withdrawal, silence and disengagement
Balanced:
- requests a clear pause;
- states a return time;
- uses solitude for regulation.

Overextended:
- becomes unreachable;
- withholds communication to regain power;
- avoids repair.

Underexpressed:
- remains in conflict while too activated to think.

### 4.11 Support seeking and co-regulation
Balanced:
- asks specifically for listening, advice, practical help or shared action;
- retains agency.

Overextended:
- seeks reassurance repeatedly without action.

Underexpressed:
- hides burden;
- reaches out only at crisis point.

### 4.12 Recovery and downshift
Balanced:
- uses repeatable recovery routines;
- distinguishes distraction from restoration;
- restores before high-stakes re-entry.

Overextended:
- turns recovery into another performance target.

Underexpressed:
- postpones recovery indefinitely;
- relies only on numbing or avoidance.

### 4.13 Repair and learning after stress behaviour
Balanced:
- names what happened;
- acknowledges impact;
- repairs harm;
- identifies trigger and alternative response.

Overextended:
- performs excessive self-punishment;
- demands immediate forgiveness.

Underexpressed:
- denies impact;
- uses stress as total excuse.

### 4.14 Cumulative load and sustainability
Balanced:
- tracks trends, not only crises;
- changes workload, support, boundaries or environment;
- names structural causes.

Overextended:
- treats every strain as a mindset failure;
- keeps optimising self inside harmful conditions.

Underexpressed:
- normalises chronic exhaustion;
- waits for collapse before change.

## 5. Conditional-shadow standard
A valid shadow record must contain:
1. trigger or context;
2. observable behaviour;
3. possible short-term function;
4. risks;
5. protective factors or alternative response.

A shadow is not a fixed hidden trait.

## 6. Eight conditional shadows

### 6.1 Impatient command mode
Triggers:
- blocked progress;
- repeated delay;
- responsibility without authority;
- time pressure.

Observable behaviours:
- short commands;
- interruption;
- compressed discussion;
- dismissing questions.

Possible function:
- restore pace and certainty.

Risks:
- missed information;
- resistance;
- reduced psychological safety.

Protective factors:
- define the real deadline;
- separate decision from discussion;
- ask for blockers;
- use a time-boxed dissent round.

### 6.2 Micromanaging control mode
Triggers:
- low trust;
- recent failure;
- unclear ownership;
- visible stakes.

Observable behaviours:
- repeated checking;
- rewriting others' work;
- approval bottlenecks;
- difficulty delegating.

Possible function:
- reduce uncertainty and prevent error.

Risks:
- overload;
- dependency;
- lower initiative in others.

Protective factors:
- define outcome and constraints;
- agree checkpoints;
- distinguish reversible from high-risk work;
- review systems rather than every action.

### 6.3 Defensive authority mode
Triggers:
- public challenge;
- correction by a lower-status person;
- loss of face;
- questioned competence.

Observable behaviours:
- overexplaining;
- arguing every detail;
- rejecting feedback;
- status reminders.

Possible function:
- protect credibility and dignity.

Risks:
- learning failure;
- trust erosion;
- escalation.

Protective factors:
- pause before response;
- separate content from delivery;
- acknowledge one specific error;
- move sensitive correction private where appropriate.

### 6.4 Overwork and indispensability mode
Triggers:
- threatened status;
- fear of failure;
- team underperformance;
- uncertain future.

Observable behaviours:
- taking over tasks;
- extended hours;
- reduced sleep;
- inability to stop.

Possible function:
- restore competence and control.

Risks:
- exhaustion;
- poor decisions;
- resentment;
- reduced team capability.

Protective factors:
- minimum viable outcome;
- workload cap;
- delegation by skill level;
- scheduled recovery.

### 6.5 Withdrawal after shame or failure
Triggers:
- visible mistake;
- rejection;
- failed confident claim;
- criticism.

Observable behaviours:
- silence;
- avoidance;
- cancelled contact;
- emotional distance.

Possible function:
- reduce exposure and protect dignity.

Risks:
- unresolved conflict;
- isolation;
- distorted assumptions.

Protective factors:
- time-bound pause;
- behaviour-specific self-review;
- trusted conversation;
- repair statement.

### 6.6 Contrarian resistance mode
Triggers:
- coercion;
- micromanagement;
- loss of choice;
- advice delivered as command.

Observable behaviours:
- reflexive disagreement;
- rejecting useful support;
- choosing the opposite to reclaim agency.

Possible function:
- restore autonomy.

Risks:
- self-defeating choices;
- missed collaboration;
- purposeless conflict.

Protective factors:
- identify the real choice boundary;
- ask for rationale;
- request options;
- decide after activation decreases.

### 6.7 Emotional compression mode
Triggers:
- belief that feelings slow action;
- role pressure to appear strong;
- fear of dependency.

Observable behaviours:
- immediate problem-solving;
- dismissing sadness or fear;
- limited emotional language;
- rapid return to work.

Possible function:
- maintain function and identity.

Risks:
- delayed escalation;
- relational disconnection;
- poor recovery.

Protective factors:
- name one emotion and one need;
- ask listening vs solutions;
- schedule later processing;
- use body-based downshift.

### 6.8 Rigid persistence mode
Triggers:
- sunk cost;
- public commitment;
- identity tied to winning;
- fear that changing course means weakness.

Observable behaviours:
- ignoring contrary evidence;
- escalating investment;
- redefining failure repeatedly;
- refusing exit criteria.

Possible function:
- preserve identity and avoid regret.

Risks:
- larger loss;
- damaged trust;
- depleted resources.

Protective factors:
- predefine stop/revise criteria;
- independent review;
- separate goal from method;
- reward course correction.

## 7. Functional stress states

### Regulated engagement
Broad attention, flexible problem-solving, proportionate tone, support access and recovery availability.

### Activated action mode
Urgency, narrowed priorities, increased control and reduced patience.

### Defensive pride mode
Status threat, feedback rejection, image protection, argument or withdrawal.

### Overcontrol mode
Micromanagement, excessive checking, delegation reversal and work intensification.

### Withdrawal mode
Silence, avoidance, reduced initiative and delayed response.

### Depleted mode
Exhaustion, reduced concentration, cynicism or detachment and poor recovery.

App rule: state labels describe current patterns, not identity or diagnosis.

## 12. Psychology boundaries

### Transactional stress and coping
Use for appraisal and coping-resource distinctions.
Do not use to imply thoughts alone cause stress or to ignore structural danger.

### Allostatic load
Use to explain cumulative strain and insufficient recovery.
Do not infer biomarkers, disease or physiological damage from app responses.

### Burnout
Use only in occupational context and without diagnosis.
Do not use as a synonym for ordinary tiredness or as a numerology prediction.

### Emotion regulation
Use to discuss timing, context, reappraisal, expression and recovery.
Do not prescribe suppression as universally adaptive.

## 13. Ten practical protocols

### 13.1 Stress appraisal reset
1. Name the event observably.
2. Identify what feels threatened.
3. Separate actual danger from status discomfort.
4. List control, influence and outside-control variables.
5. Choose one proportional action.

### 13.2 Response delay
For non-emergency conflict:
1. stop speaking or sending;
2. reduce bodily activation;
3. identify the intended outcome;
4. check reversibility;
5. respond when tone matches purpose.

### 13.3 Control-circle map
Create three columns: direct control, influence and outside control. Move effort toward the first two.

### 13.4 Command-to-request conversion
Convert accusation to observation, unnecessary command to request, assumption to question and vague urgency to a concrete deadline.

### 13.5 Time-bound withdrawal
State activation, request a pause, specify a return time, avoid punitive silence and re-enter with summary and repair.

### 13.6 Delegation under stress
Define outcome, constraints, checkpoints and escalation threshold. Do not reclaim work because style differs.

### 13.7 Shame and failure recovery
Describe the specific error, separate behaviour from identity, identify impact, repair, define one changed behaviour and return to participation.

### 13.8 Workload and recovery audit
Review hours, intensity, sleep, role clarity, support, control, recovery and structural change needs.

### 13.9 Stop/revise criteria
Before major effort, define success evidence, unacceptable downside, review date and pause/revise/stop conditions.

### 13.10 Support request script
“I am dealing with ____. I need listening, advice, practical help, shared decision-making or professional support. What I still want to own is ____.”

## 21. Conflict and limitation notes
1. Stress models emphasise different mechanisms: appraisal, physiology, environment, learning and social context.
2. Fight/flight/freeze language can oversimplify mixed responses.
3. Suppression may be costly in many contexts but can sometimes be strategically useful; context matters.
4. Control can be adaptive when risk is real and harmful when expanded beyond scope.
5. Burnout definitions and measurement remain heterogeneous.
6. Culture affects emotional expression, support seeking, authority and disagreement.
7. Structural stressors cannot be solved only through individual coping.
8. No reviewed source establishes birth-number prediction of stress style.

## 23. Canonical conclusion
The app-safe Moolank 1 stress model is not “an aggressive or controlling person under pressure.” It is a conditional reflection architecture:
- blocked agency may increase urgency;
- uncertainty may increase control-seeking;
- public correction may activate defensive pride;
- overload may produce overwork, withdrawal or reduced flexibility;
- structure, recovery, role clarity, feedback safety and early support can reduce escalation.

Every output must remain conditional, observable, correctable and non-diagnostic. Stress patterns must be explained through current behaviour and context, not treated as proof that numerology predicts personality.
