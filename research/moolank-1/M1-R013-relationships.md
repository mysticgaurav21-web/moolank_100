## 1. Purpose

This unit translates traditional Number 1 themes—independence, initiative, pride, leadership, recognition sensitivity and self-direction—into a safe, conditional relationship-reflection framework.

It does **not** predict partner choice, loyalty, attachment style, marriage quality, sexual compatibility, divorce, abuse, family role or relationship outcome.

Every app output must be:

1. optional rather than fixed;
2. conditional rather than universal;
3. behaviour-focused rather than identity-labelling;
4. compatible with mutual dignity and agency;
5. subordinate to real-world evidence and safety;
6. separate from diagnosis.

## 2. Core scientific boundary

There is no established scientific evidence that a birth-day number predicts:

- attachment security;
- emotional availability;
- trustworthiness;
- loyalty or infidelity;
- jealousy;
- controlling behaviour;
- violence or victimisation;
- sexual behaviour;
- marriage duration;
- separation or reconciliation;
- partner suitability.

Psychology and relationship science are used only to define constructs and safe practices. They do not validate numerology.

## 3. Construct definitions

### 3.1 Autonomy

Autonomy is acting from self-endorsed values and choices while remaining open to mutual influence.

Autonomy is not emotional distance, secrecy, unilateral control, refusal to compromise or avoidance of support.

### 3.2 Independence

Independence is the capacity to manage some needs, tasks and decisions without excessive reliance on another person.

Independence can coexist with intimacy.

### 3.3 Interdependence

Interdependence is coordinated mutual reliance in which both people retain identity, agency, boundaries and access to outside support.

Interdependence is not fusion, compulsory disclosure, constant availability or one-sided caretaking.

### 3.4 Closeness

Closeness includes emotional access, trust, responsiveness, shared meaning, affection and willingness to be known.

Closeness is not ownership or unrestricted access.

### 3.5 Attachment language

Attachment constructs describe patterns of expectation, emotion and behaviour in close relationships. They require careful assessment and are not birth-number labels.

The app must not infer secure, anxious, avoidant, dismissive, fearful or disorganised attachment from numerology or a short quiz.

It may discuss observable behaviours such as reassurance seeking, conflict withdrawal, discomfort depending on others, fear of rejection or fear of lost autonomy.

### 3.6 Trust

Trust is a context-specific willingness to accept vulnerability based on evidence of honesty, reliability, care, confidentiality and repair.

Trust is not blind faith and can be limited, rebuilt gradually or withdrawn.

### 3.7 Boundaries

A boundary states what a person will accept, decline, protect or do.

A boundary is not a command that removes another person’s agency. Consequences must be proportionate and within the boundary-setter’s control.

### 3.8 Conflict

Conflict is disagreement, incompatible preference, unmet need or competing interpretation.

Conflict is not automatically abuse. Abuse involves behaviour-specific patterns such as coercion, intimidation, violence, sexual coercion, stalking, isolation, threats, surveillance or financial restriction.

### 3.9 Repair

Repair includes acknowledging impact, accepting relevant responsibility, changing behaviour and rebuilding reliability.

Repair is not merely apologising, demanding forgiveness or returning to normal without change.

## 5. Dimension deep dives

### 5.1 Selfhood within closeness

**Balanced**

- retains personal values, interests and relationships;
- invests in shared identity without self-erasure;
- tolerates difference without treating it as rejection;
- supports the other person’s development;
- distinguishes love from sameness.

**Overextended**

- protects individuality through distance or unilateral action;
- treats coordination as loss of freedom;
- withholds plans to avoid discussion;
- dismisses needs as dependency;
- uses identity as protection from accountability.

**Underexpressed**

- abandons preferences to maintain approval;
- loses independent goals or support networks;
- agrees outwardly while resenting privately;
- relies on the relationship to define identity;
- interprets differentiation as disloyalty.

**Prompt:** What part of this choice is individual, shared or owned by the other person?

### 5.2 Autonomy–connection balance

**Balanced**

- requests space without threatening the bond;
- requests closeness without demanding constant access;
- negotiates privacy and contact;
- distinguishes chosen care from forced compliance;
- can depend on another person without surrendering judgment.

**Overextended**

- avoids vulnerable dependence;
- withdraws whenever needs become complex;
- defines compromise as defeat;
- insists on unilateral control of shared matters;
- ignores the relational cost of absence.

**Underexpressed**

- seeks permission for ordinary choices;
- remains constantly available from fear;
- suppresses the need for space;
- accepts intrusion to prove love;
- cannot tolerate separate activity.

**Prompt:** What amount of contact, space and coordination preserves both connection and choice?

### 5.3 Mutual influence

**Balanced**

- listens before deciding;
- changes position when evidence or values justify it;
- invites input on decisions with shared consequences;
- preserves individual decision ownership where appropriate;
- distinguishes influence from domination.

**Overextended**

- treats influence as a challenge to authority;
- seeks agreement rather than understanding;
- interrupts or solves before listening;
- dismisses emotional information;
- uses status, expertise or income as a permanent veto.

**Underexpressed**

- gives away decisions to avoid responsibility;
- over-adapts to stronger personalities;
- hides disagreement;
- asks for advice without stating a preference;
- confuses harmony with consent.

**Prompt:** Is this decision individual, consultative, joint or delegated?

### 5.4 Trust calibration

**Balanced**

- allows trust to grow through evidence;
- verifies important information without surveillance;
- notices reliability and breaches;
- sets proportionate safeguards after harm;
- distinguishes uncertainty from proof of betrayal.

**Overextended**

- demands access to devices or accounts;
- treats independence as secrecy;
- repeatedly interrogates without new evidence;
- expects obedience as proof of loyalty;
- uses jealousy to justify control.

**Underexpressed**

- ignores repeated breaches;
- confuses forgiveness with restored trust;
- accepts explanations without behavioural change;
- avoids necessary questions;
- continues investing despite persistent unreliability.

**Prompt:** What specific behaviour increases or decreases trust?

### 5.5 Emotional responsiveness

**Balanced**

- asks whether the person wants listening, comfort, ideas or action;
- validates experience without automatically agreeing with every interpretation;
- responds to bids for connection when able;
- communicates temporary unavailability and returns;
- adapts support to the recipient.

**Overextended**

- immediately problem-solves;
- minimises emotion to restore efficiency;
- interprets distress as accusation;
- becomes defensive when care is requested;
- provides help in a controlling way.

**Underexpressed**

- absorbs the other person’s emotion as a personal duty;
- cannot set limits on support;
- overfunctions to prevent disappointment;
- reassures beyond evidence;
- avoids honest disagreement to maintain calm.

**Prompt:** Does this moment need presence, information, practical help, a boundary or professional support?

### 5.6 Support giving

**Balanced**

- asks consent before advising;
- makes support specific;
- avoids creating obligation through help;
- respects refusal;
- shares workload transparently.

**Overextended**

- takes over without agreement;
- expects gratitude, loyalty or control in return;
- uses resources to direct choices;
- frames the partner as incapable;
- confuses helping with managing.

**Underexpressed**

- notices need but avoids offering help;
- assumes competence means no care is required;
- withholds help during conflict;
- contributes only where visible or rewarded;
- treats shared burdens as individual failures.

### 5.7 Support receiving

**Balanced**

- names needs directly;
- accepts help without self-devaluation;
- expresses appreciation;
- retains decision responsibility;
- reciprocates according to capacity.

**Overextended**

- refuses help to protect pride;
- hides difficulty until crisis;
- interprets care as pity or control;
- accepts help only on rigid terms;
- criticises the method while ignoring contribution.

**Underexpressed**

- expects rescue without clear requests;
- delegates emotional regulation entirely to the partner;
- treats constant availability as proof of love;
- uses distress to override boundaries;
- avoids developing personal capacity.

### 5.8 Affection and appreciation

**Balanced**

- appreciates specific behaviour and effort;
- gives credit without losing status;
- receives affection without deflection;
- notices invisible labour;
- adapts expression to consent and preference.

**Overextended**

- expects admiration as proof of loyalty;
- becomes resentful when effort is unnoticed;
- competes for recognition;
- uses praise strategically to secure compliance;
- equates public display with relationship quality.

**Underexpressed**

- assumes appreciation is obvious;
- feels affection but rarely communicates it;
- rejects praise from discomfort;
- minimises contribution;
- withholds warmth during minor disagreement.

### 5.9 Conflict engagement

**Balanced**

- raises issues while they remain specific;
- separates behaviour from identity;
- regulates intensity;
- hears the other view before rebuttal;
- pauses with a return plan;
- seeks workable resolution rather than total victory.

**Overextended**

- dominates airtime;
- uses certainty, volume or status to win;
- treats disagreement as disrespect;
- imports unrelated past failures;
- forces immediate resolution;
- threatens the relationship to secure compliance.

**Underexpressed**

- avoids necessary conversation;
- agrees while planning not to comply;
- withdraws without a return plan;
- stores grievances until rupture;
- accepts unfair outcomes to end discomfort.

### 5.10 Power and decision-sharing

**Balanced**

- identifies who is affected;
- separates expertise from permanent authority;
- makes financial and household arrangements visible;
- protects the lower-power person’s ability to disagree;
- shares credit and accountability.

**Overextended**

- uses income, age, gender, status or family role as entitlement;
- controls money or information;
- requires permission for ordinary activities;
- punishes dissent;
- makes shared decisions unilaterally;
- monitors communication or movement.

**Underexpressed**

- avoids responsibility;
- lets one person carry all planning and consequences;
- hides preferences until after decisions;
- expects mind-reading;
- remains silent in unequal arrangements.

### 5.11 Boundary communication

**Balanced**

- states limits before resentment escalates;
- focuses on behaviour;
- names what action will be taken;
- respects the other person’s right to decline or leave;
- updates boundaries when circumstances change.

**Overextended**

- presents preferences as moral rules;
- calls commands “boundaries”;
- uses silence, money or access as punishment;
- tries to control another person’s independent choices;
- changes standards unpredictably.

**Underexpressed**

- says yes while feeling no;
- relies on hints;
- tolerates repeated intrusion;
- sets limits only after explosive anger;
- abandons boundaries when challenged.

### 5.12 Vulnerability and disclosure

**Balanced**

- discloses gradually according to trust;
- names fear, hurt, need or uncertainty without blame;
- allows an imperfect response;
- protects confidential information;
- distinguishes honesty from compulsory total disclosure.

**Overextended**

- avoids disclosure to preserve competence or status;
- replaces feeling with instruction;
- reveals only after escalation;
- interprets dependence as weakness;
- later weaponises private information.

**Underexpressed**

- discloses intensely before trust is established;
- demands immediate reciprocal disclosure;
- uses vulnerability to prevent boundaries or separation;
- expects the partner to regulate every feeling;
- treats privacy as betrayal.

### 5.13 Rupture and repair

**Balanced**

- names the action and impact;
- accepts responsibility without self-destruction;
- listens to what repair means;
- proposes observable change;
- allows trust to rebuild at the harmed person’s pace;
- recognises when reconciliation is unsafe or unwanted.

**Overextended**

- explains intent instead of addressing impact;
- seeks quick forgiveness;
- apologises repeatedly without change;
- demands equal blame before taking responsibility;
- uses gifts or affection to bypass accountability.

**Underexpressed**

- accepts blame for harm not caused;
- apologises only to stop conflict;
- returns to normal without discussion;
- forgives under pressure;
- remains in unsafe situations because repair is promised.

### 5.14 Relationship safety and exit autonomy

Each person retains the right to bodily autonomy, sexual consent, privacy, support, movement, resources and the ability to leave.

Behaviour-specific safety indicators include:

- physical violence or threats;
- sexual pressure or forced activity;
- threats used to prevent separation;
- stalking or non-consensual location monitoring;
- isolation from friends, family or work;
- confiscation of money, documents, phone or transport;
- intimidation, humiliation or repeated degradation;
- reproductive control;
- property destruction;
- threats involving children, pets, reputation or immigration status;
- escalating fear.

When credible fear, coercion or violence is present, the app must stop mutual-communication coaching and prioritise safety routing.

## 6. Functional relationship states

### 6.1 Connected autonomy

Clear selfhood, mutual influence, reciprocal care, evidence-based trust, direct requests, proportionate boundaries and repair capacity.

### 6.2 Directive overfunctioning

One person plans, decides or solves too much. Support becomes management and efficiency replaces consultation.

Growth direction: clarify decision rights, ask before helping and tolerate alternative methods.

### 6.3 Defensive independence

Needs are hidden, help is refused, closeness is interpreted as control and vulnerability is delayed.

Growth direction: bounded disclosure, specific support requests and differentiation between present behaviour and past threat.

### 6.4 Recognition conflict

Appreciation becomes scarce or competitive, criticism feels like rejection and both people defend contribution.

Growth direction: specific appreciation, workload visibility and behaviour-focused feedback.

### 6.5 Pursuit–withdrawal loop

One person increases demands for contact or resolution while the other increases distance. Each reaction strengthens the other’s fear.

Growth direction: slow the loop, name fear and need, schedule return and avoid forced resolution.

The app must not diagnose attachment styles from this pattern.

### 6.6 Conflict avoidance and resentment

Surface agreement, indirect resistance, delayed limits and sudden rupture.

Growth direction: early low-intensity disagreement, clear preferences and regular review of shared arrangements.

### 6.7 Coercive or unsafe pattern

Fear, domination, isolation, surveillance, threats, violence, sexual coercion, financial restriction or blocked exit.

Growth direction is not mutual compromise. Safety information and qualified local support take priority.

## 9. Context matrix

### Romantic relationships

Closeness, autonomy, affection, consent, trust, exclusivity agreements, shared planning and repair.

Never predict marriage, separation, fidelity or sexual compatibility.

### Marriage and cohabitation

Financial transparency, labour, caregiving, family boundaries, shared property, decision rights and exit autonomy.

Cultural roles never override consent or safety.

### Family relationships

Intergenerational respect, differentiation, caregiving limits, adult autonomy and resource conflict.

Reconciliation is not always safe or required.

### Friendship

Reciprocity, contact expectations, loyalty without possession, appreciation, repair and separate networks.

Lower contact does not automatically mean rejection.

### Workplace relationships

Role boundaries, feedback, authority, credit, support, conflict and professional privacy.

Workplace hierarchy must not be analysed as romantic attachment.

### Long-distance relationships

Communication agreements, uncertainty, independent routines, planning, digital privacy and trust evidence.

Constant tracking is not trust-building.

### Post-separation relationships

Boundaries, logistics, co-parenting where applicable, digital access, financial separation, stalking and safety.

Do not pressure reconciliation or joint communication when unsafe.

## 10. Psychology boundary layer

### Attachment theory

Attachment research examines expectations, emotion and behaviour in close relationships. Constructs are history- and context-sensitive.

Safe use:

- discuss comfort with closeness, reassurance, dependence and conflict;
- observe current behaviour;
- suggest professional assessment when distress is significant.

Unsafe use:

- label attachment style from birth date;
- portray style as destiny;
- use attachment language to excuse coercion;
- diagnose an absent partner.

### Perceived partner responsiveness

A useful general construct is whether a person feels understood, cared for and valued in observable ways.

Safe prompt: “Do you feel heard and cared for in behaviour you can identify?”

### Demand–withdraw dynamics

Some distressed relationships show loops in which one person presses for discussion while another withdraws.

Safe use: identify the loop, slow escalation, establish a return time and distinguish pause from abandonment.

Unsafe use: blame one gender, diagnose avoidance or apply mutual coaching when discussion is unsafe.

### Autonomy support

Autonomy support includes acknowledging perspective, offering meaningful choice and reducing unnecessary control.

Safe use: requests rather than commands, shared decision rights and protection of agency.

### Conflict and repair

Conflict quality matters more than the existence of disagreement. Useful practices include clear issue definition, reduced contempt, listening, time-outs, accountability and changed behaviour.

No single technique guarantees success.

### Coercive control and intimate partner violence

Controlling behaviour, sexual coercion, violence, threats, economic restriction and isolation are safety issues—not numerology themes.

The app must protect privacy, avoid encouraging confrontation and route to local support when fear or danger is indicated.

## 13. Practical protocols

### 13.1 Decision-right map

Classify an issue as individual, inform-only, consultative, joint or delegated. Define who decides, who is consulted and who carries consequences.

### 13.2 Listening–ideas–action check

Ask whether the person wants listening, comfort, ideas, practical action or a decision.

### 13.3 Observation–impact–request

Use specific behaviour, describe impact and make a concrete negotiable request.

### 13.4 Conflict pause with return

A safe pause includes a reason, approximate return time, no punishment or disappearance and actual resumption unless safety requires otherwise.

### 13.5 Appreciation specificity

Name the action, effort, value expressed and effect.

### 13.6 Trust ledger

Track agreements kept, information accuracy, confidentiality, repair and consistency over time. Do not use this for surveillance or point-scoring.

### 13.7 Boundary script

“I am not willing to participate in X. If X occurs, I will do Y.”

Y must remain within the speaker’s control.

### 13.8 Repair protocol

1. State what happened.
2. Name the impact heard.
3. Accept relevant responsibility.
4. Avoid immediate counter-accusation.
5. Ask what repair would help.
6. Propose observable change.
7. Review whether change occurred.

### 13.9 Support-receiving ladder

Name the problem, specify desired support, accept a small contribution, retain decision ownership, express appreciation and review fit.

### 13.10 Safety-first override

When fear, threats, violence, sexual coercion, stalking, isolation or blocked resources are indicated:

- stop mutual communication coaching;
- do not encourage confrontation;
- protect privacy;
- present local emergency and specialist support;
- allow quick exit;
- avoid unnecessary storage of disclosures.

## 14. Progress indicators

Possible non-clinical indicators:

- needs are stated earlier and more specifically;
- requests are less command-like;
- both people retain independent support;
- disagreements occur without fear or punishment;
- decision rights are clearer;
- support is offered with consent;
- help can be received without shame;
- appreciation becomes specific and reciprocal;
- pauses include reliable return;
- apologies include behavioural change;
- trust is updated through evidence;
- boundaries become clearer;
- unsafe patterns are routed to support rather than compatibility analysis.

These are not a relationship score and must not determine whether someone stays or leaves.

## 20. Source conflicts and resolution

### Independence as strength vs distance as risk

Resolution: evaluate whether independence supports chosen self-direction while preserving care, honesty and shared responsibility.

### Compromise vs authenticity

Resolution: compromise is useful for shared matters but cannot erase consent, safety or identity.

### Persistence vs staying in harm

Resolution: commitment never justifies remaining in violence, coercion or credible danger.

### Couple responsibility vs power asymmetry

Resolution: ordinary conflict may involve both people, but coercive control must not be reduced to symmetrical communication problems.

### Privacy vs transparency

Resolution: shared matters may require transparency while personal privacy remains legitimate. Surveillance is not transparency.

### Attachment insight vs labelling

Resolution: discuss present behaviours and needs; do not assign styles from numerology or limited data.
